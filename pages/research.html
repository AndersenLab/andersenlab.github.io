What we Study

Model organisms provide the opportunity to experimentally test the correlations between genes and disease-related processes because of the high-level of conservation and the ease of manipulation. However, most model organism research is based on a single wild-type strain background with little connection to natural variation, which is like studying a single person to make conclusions about the entire human species. C. elegans is isolated worldwide and has genetic variation comparable to that of humans. Therefore, C. elegans provides the opportunity to identify the genes that vary among individuals and the molecular mechanisms for how genetic variation causes phenotypic differences.

Projects

High-throughput approaches to understand conserved drug responses

We use novel pipelines to accurately measure offspring production, growth rate, and muscle activity in diverse nematode strains. These results allow us to rapidly map variant genes underlying responses to chemotherapeutics, anthelmintics, neuroactive compounds, pesticides, and heavy metals. Learn More

Species-wide and genome-wide identification of genomic variants

We have a data set of nearly every strain of C. elegans sequenced to 50X coverage. These data will be used to identify diverse classes of variation, including single nucleotides, insertions/deletions, micro satellites, transposon insertions, genomic rearrangements, and copy-number variants. Such data will help narrow our genetic mapping experiments to candidate genes and teach us about population genetic forces shaping the C. elegans genome. Learn More

Identification of the genes that underlie gene-by-environment (GxE) interactions

We use our high-throughput assays and standardized environmental perturbations to look at how organisms respond to altered environments. We measure responses to altered growth temperatures and the bacterial food sources. Learn More

Investigation of natural variation in conserved signaling pathways

We are interested in how conserved signaling pathways vary among individuals of a population. This variation is likely important for differences in human disease incidences, including cancer and diabetes. Learn More
